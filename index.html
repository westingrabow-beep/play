Pillar 1 of 4

Rent over Profit

—

The feudalism case

Income is shifting from labor and productive capital toward rent extraction. The US labor share has fallen to its lowest level since records began in 1947. Corporate profit share has nearly doubled since 1982.

The skeptic's rebuttal

Labor-share decline has many causes: automation, globalization, union decline, and the rise of intangible capital. A declining labor share is consistent with capitalism — it does not require feudalism. Rent has always coexisted with profit.

Sources: BLS Labor Productivity & Costs (Q3 2025, via Fortune Jan 2026); Karabarbounis (2024), Journal of Economic Perspectives; KPMG / Diane Swonk (via Fortune Feb 2026). Real data points shown as filled circles; interpolated values as lighter lines. Vintage: data through 2025.

US labor share: 53.8% (Q3 2025, record low since 1947 series began). Down from 67.8% in 1987 — a 14-point fall over 38 years. Corporate profit share: ~15.85% of GDP (2024), up from ~8% in 1982.

The crux of the debate

This slider is where the entire argument turns. Varoufakis: the gains above normal profit rates are economic rent, extracted via market power. Mainstream economists: much of the gain reflects fair returns on intangible investment (brands, software, data infrastructure). Neither claim is directly falsifiable from share-of-GDP data alone.
Pillar 2 of 4

Markets to Fiefdoms

—

The feudalism case

Open markets replaced by walled platforms charging mandatory access tolls. App Store and Play take 30% of every transaction. Amazon's blended take-rate on third-party sellers runs ~50% when fees, ads, and logistics stack. A medieval lord taxing the miller at 30% is structurally identical.

The skeptic's rebuttal

Platforms still compete with each other. Apple competes with Android. Amazon competes with Shopify, Walmart, direct-to-consumer. High take-rates reflect the value of customer access, trust infrastructure, and logistics — not extraction from captive vassals. Exit is possible; switching costs, not feudal bonds.

App Store / Google Play: standard 30% commission (15% for small developers & subscriptions after year 1). Amazon blended take-rate: estimate 45–51% (Varoufakis cites ~51%; marketplace-fee studies: high 40s–50%). Open e-commerce (Shopify + payment): ~3–5%. Sources: Apple Developer docs; Amazon seller fee schedules; California DSA review of Varoufakis. Label: estimate for Amazon figure.

Pillar 3 of 4

Concentration of Cloud Capital

—

The feudalism case

The top-10 S&P 500 companies now hold ~39% of the index's market cap — higher than the dot-com peak of 27%. The Magnificent 7 generate ~70% of the S&P 500's economic profit. Three firms (AWS, Azure, GCP) control ~65% of global cloud infrastructure. This is the "cloud capital" Varoufakis identifies as the new means of production.

The skeptic's rebuttal

Concentration is historically cyclical. The railroads in 1900, the Nifty Fifty in the 1960s–70s, the dot-com era — all resolved without feudalism. Today's top-10 P/E (~31) is elevated but below the 2000 peak (~43). Earnings justify much of the valuation; this is monopoly capitalism, not feudalism.

Sources: Goldman Sachs; Columbia Threadneedle; AFM&M; Motley Fool; FinancialContent. Top-10 share 2026: ~38–40% (early 2026). Dot-com peak 2000: ~27%. Top-10 P/E ~31 vs. ~21 for rest of index (vs. 43 vs. 21 in 2000). Vintage: early 2026.

Historical concentration episodes

1900s US railroads: top carriers controlled ~70% of freight. Resolved via regulation (ICC), not systemic collapse. 1960s Nifty Fifty: 50 stocks at P/Es of 40–90×. Corrected 1973–74. 2000 dot-com: top-10 at 27% of S&P; P/E 43×. Today: 39%, P/E 31×. Elevated — but with earnings that partly justify the premium.
Mag-7 economic profit share: ~70% of S&P 500 (Russell Investments via AFM&M). Top-10 earnings share: ~31% of total index. The concentration in profits exceeds the concentration in market cap — which is precisely Varoufakis's point about monopolistic rent, and the skeptic's point about genuine productivity gains.
Pillar 4 of 4

Serfs & Vassals

—
Weakest evidence. This pillar is mostly assumption — you supply the numbers. There is no audited, cross-jurisdictional dataset on the share of platform value derived from user-generated content or small-firm dependence. Treat this pillar as a sensitivity test, not empirical measurement. The teaching point: the rhetorically most powerful pillar is the least empirically grounded.

The feudalism case

Users generate the data and content that makes platforms valuable without compensation — structurally identical to unpaid serf labor. Small firms are so dependent on Amazon, Meta, or Google for demand that they cannot operate independently: they are "cloud vassals," paying tribute to survive.

The skeptic's rebuttal

Users receive services in exchange — search, social graphs, logistics networks — which is a bilateral transaction, not extraction. The "unpaid labor" framing is ideological, not accounting. Small firms chose platform distribution because it was more efficient, not because they were coerced. They retain the option to exit.

Methodology

Sources & Caveats

Data sources

  • Labor share (BLS series): US Bureau of Labor Statistics, Labor Productivity & Costs, nonfarm business sector. Q3 2025 value: 53.8% (prior quarter 54.6%; 2020s avg ~55.6%). Via Fortune, Jan 2026. Peak: 67.8% in 1987 (Karabarbounis 2024, Journal of Economic Perspectives).
  • Corporate profit / compensation shares: KPMG (Diane Swonk) via Fortune, Feb 2026. Compensation: ~66.6% (1982) → ~61.9% (2024). Profits: ~8% (1982) → ~15.85% (2024) as share of GDP. Note: this uses a different base (national income) than the BLS nonfarm series — the two measures are not directly comparable.
  • Profit share historical range: PGIM Fixed Income, "Evolution of US Corporate Profits." ~16% (1950–70); ~17% through 2000; ~22% post-GFC. This appears to measure corporate profits as a share of corporate sector value-added, which differs from BEA profit/GDP; treat as directional.
  • Fortune 500 profits: Fortune 500 annual list. Record $1.87 trillion in 2024.
  • Platform take-rates: Apple Developer Program License Agreement; Amazon Seller Central fee schedules (FBA + referral + ads blended). Amazon figure (~50%) is an estimate with range 45–51%; labeled accordingly. California DSA review of Varoufakis (2024).
  • S&P 500 concentration: Goldman Sachs (top-10 P/E, market cap share); Columbia Threadneedle; AFM&M. Top-10 share in early 2026: ~38–40%. Dot-com peak 2000: ~27%. 2020 figure: ~22%. Mag-7 economic profit share (~70%): Russell Investments via AFM&M.

What is measured vs. modeled

  • Measured (real data): Labor share trend; corporate profit share; platform take-rates; S&P 500 concentration ratios. Shown with filled-circle markers on charts.
  • Interpolated: Annual data between known anchor points on Pillar 1 and 3 charts. Shown with lighter lines. Interpolation is linear; actual data varies. Treat as illustrative, not precise.
  • Modeled / assumption-driven: All Pillar 4 inputs; the "rent vs. productive return" fraction on Pillar 1; the "framing" toggle on Pillar 2; the "feudal threshold" on Pillar 3. These represent interpretive choices, not empirical findings.

Important limitations

  • The Techno-Feudalism Index is a diagnostic heuristic, not a rigorous economic index. The scoring functions are designed to be transparent and adjustable, not to assert a single true value.
  • The "labor share decline has many causes" caveat is real and important: automation and intangible capital investment can reduce labor share within competitive capitalism. The slider makes the user own their causal assumption.
  • US data predominates. The labor-share decline is documented across OECD economies (IMF, 2017; ILO, various) but with significant cross-country variation. EU and Global series on the chart are illustrative only.
  • The Amazon take-rate figure is a blended estimate contested by Amazon. The company disputes the "~50%" characterization. The range 45–51% reflects independent analysis of fee schedules.
