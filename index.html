Are We Leaving Capitalism?

An explorer for a big, controversial idea

The big idea: What if capitalism is slowly turning into something like feudalism — where a handful of tech giants act like medieval landlords, charging everyone else "rent" just to do business online? Topic 1 of 4

Who Gets the Money?

—

The feudalism argument

Workers are getting a smaller share of what they produce. In 1987, workers received about 68 cents out of every dollar of output. By 2025, that fell to just 54 cents. Meanwhile, big-company profits nearly doubled. Varoufakis says this shift toward profit — especially "rent" from monopoly power — looks a lot like feudalism.

The counter-argument

Workers' declining share has many explanations that have nothing to do with feudalism: automation, globalization, and the rise of tech companies that produce a lot with few employees. Profits going up doesn't automatically mean exploitation. Capitalism has always had winners and losers.

Sources: BLS Labor Productivity & Costs (Q3 2025, via Fortune Jan 2026); Karabarbounis (2024), Journal of Economic Perspectives; KPMG / Diane Swonk (via Fortune Feb 2026). Solid dots = real measured data. Lines = estimated trend between measurements. Data through 2025.

Workers' share of output hit 53.8% in Q3 2025 — the lowest recorded since 1947. It was 67.8% in 1987. Big-company profits as % of GDP: ~16% in 2024, up from ~8% in 1982.

The key question

This slider is where the whole argument lives. Varoufakis says excess profit is basically a tax on everyone else — like a lord taxing his peasants. Mainstream economists say it reflects fair returns on software, brands, and data. Neither side can fully prove their case with this data alone.
Topic 2 of 4

Are Platforms Like Landlords?

—

The feudalism argument

To sell anything online today, you almost have to go through Amazon, Apple, or Google — and they take 30–50% of every sale, not for making your product, but just for letting you use their "marketplace." A medieval lord who charged the village baker to use his oven worked the same way. That's rent, not fair competition.

The counter-argument

These platforms still compete with each other. Sellers can also use Shopify, Walmart, or sell directly. The fees are high because they provide real value: trusted customers, secure payments, and fast shipping. Sellers chose to be there — nobody forced them. High fees aren't the same as captivity.

App Store / Google Play: 30% standard commission (15% for small developers). Amazon blended take-rate: ~45–51% estimated when you add fees, ads, and shipping. Open e-commerce (Shopify + payment): ~3–5%. Sources: Apple Developer docs; Amazon Seller Central fee schedules; California DSA review of Varoufakis. Amazon disputes the ~50% figure.

Topic 3 of 4

Are a Few Companies Too Dominant?

—

The feudalism argument

The top 10 companies in the S&P 500 now own ~39% of the entire stock market's value — higher than even the dot-com bubble peak. Just seven companies (the "Magnificent 7") generate about 70% of all S&P 500 economic profit. Varoufakis says whoever controls this digital infrastructure controls the economy the way feudal lords controlled the land.

The counter-argument

History is full of "too big" moments — railroads in 1900, oil companies in the 1920s, conglomerates in the 1970s. They all faded or got regulated. Today's giants have strong earnings that partly justify their size. Concentration doesn't automatically mean feudalism — it might just be monopoly capitalism (which is different and fixable).

Sources: Goldman Sachs; Columbia Threadneedle; AFM&M; Motley Fool. Top-10 S&P 500 share in early 2026: ~38–40%. Dot-com peak in 2000: ~27%. The dashed line shows your custom "too concentrated" threshold — drag the slider to set it.

It has happened before

1900s railroads: top carriers controlled ~70% of US freight. Broken up by government regulation. 1970s Nifty Fifty: 50 stocks at 40–90× earnings. Crashed 1973–74. Dot-com 2000: top-10 at 27% of S&P, P/E ratio of 43×. Today: 39%, P/E of 31×. Elevated — but earnings are more solid than 2000.
The Magnificent 7 earn ~70% of all economic profit in the S&P 500 (Russell Investments). The concentration in profits is even higher than their share of the stock market — that's Varoufakis's main point. Skeptics call it "just being really productive."
Topic 4 of 4

Are Users and Small Businesses Trapped?

—
Heads up — this section is mostly your opinion, not hard facts. There's no reliable public dataset that measures exactly how "trapped" users or small businesses are. You're supplying the numbers. The point is to test your own assumptions — and see how much the score changes when you do.

The feudalism argument

Every photo you post, every review you write, every click you make — that's free work making platforms worth billions. Varoufakis compares users to medieval serfs who farm the lord's land without pay. Small businesses that rely entirely on Amazon or Google for their customers are "cloud vassals" — paying tribute just to survive.

The counter-argument

You get something real in return: free search, free social networks, free maps, free email. That's a trade, not exploitation. And small businesses chose platforms because they worked — nobody forced them. Building a website and advertising elsewhere was always an option. Choosing convenience isn't the same as being a serf.

Behind the Numbers

Sources & Limitations

Where the data comes from

  • Workers' share of output (BLS series): US Bureau of Labor Statistics, nonfarm business sector. Q3 2025 value: 53.8% (prior quarter: 54.6%). Via Fortune, Jan 2026. Peak: 67.8% in 1987 (Karabarbounis 2024, Journal of Economic Perspectives).
  • Corporate profits: KPMG (Diane Swonk) via Fortune, Feb 2026. Profits as % of GDP: ~8% in 1982 → ~15.85% in 2024. Note: different measurement methods produce different numbers — treat trends as directional.
  • Platform take-rates: Apple Developer Program License Agreement; Amazon Seller Central fee schedules (FBA + referral + ads blended). Amazon's ~50% figure is an estimate (range 45–51%); Amazon disputes this characterization.
  • S&P 500 concentration: Goldman Sachs; Columbia Threadneedle; AFM&M. Top-10 share in early 2026: ~38–40%. Dot-com peak 2000: ~27%. Mag-7 economic profit share (~70%): Russell Investments via AFM&M.

What's real data vs. what's your opinion

  • Real measured data: Workers' income share, corporate profits, platform fees, stock market concentration. Shown as solid dots on charts.
  • Estimated trend lines: Values between measured data points. Shown as lighter lines. Treat as approximate — they connect real points, they don't add new information.
  • Your opinion: All of Topic 4 (users & sellers trapped), the "unfair vs. fair profit" slider (Topic 1), the "fair fee vs. toll" toggle (Topic 2), and the concentration threshold (Topic 3). These are your assumptions — not established facts.

Important warnings

  • The Techno-Feudalism Index is a thinking tool, not a scientific measurement. It's designed to make you aware of your own assumptions — not to give you a "correct" answer.
  • All data is US-focused. Other countries show similar trends but with significant differences.
  • Workers' income share can fall for many reasons that aren't about exploitation: automation, more part-time work, the rise of asset-light tech companies that produce a lot with few employees.
  • The book this tool is based on: Yanis Varoufakis, Technofeudalism: What Killed Capitalism (2023). The counter-arguments draw on mainstream economic critiques of that thesis.
